*Presentation*
A typical FYP project presentation contains the following contents:
1. Introduction to the Project
      • Provides context to your project and gives an overview of what you're about to present. Keep it concise and engaging 
         to capture the assessor's attention early on. You could also include a brief background of why this project is 
         important or relevant in the current industry or research context.
2. Problem Statement
     • Clearly define the problem you're addressing. This sets the stage for why your project exists. Focus on making the 
         problem relatable, either by explaining its impact on a specific user group or industry. Make sure it is 
        easily understandable to your assessor.
3. Objective
   • Outlining your project's objectives is crucial to show what you aim to achieve. Highlight how achieving these 
       objectives will solve the problem or add value. You can also mention the measurable outcomes you expect 
       (e.g., increased efficiency, reduced costs, etc.).
4. Research Done
   • Demonstrating that you've researched existing solutions or background information strengthens the validity 
       of your project. Focus on the most relevant research and how it influenced your approach or highlighted gaps 
       in existing solutions. You can mention briefly if there are any novel aspects in your solution that existing research 
      doesn't address.

5. The Solution
    • Explaining your solution is the core of your presentation. This is where you show how you've tackled the 
       problem. Break down your solution into key components or features. Use diagrams (e.g., system 
       architecture) to visually communicate the structure of your solution. Keep the explanation as straightforward 
       as possible.
6. The Algorithm
     • Poviding the algorithm gives insight into the technical depth of your solution. Provide a simplified the 
         explanation (you only have 30 minutes to do your presentation and demonstration.) Focus on the high-level 
        logic or approach. You can briefly show code snippets or pseudocode, if necessary, but don't dive too deep 
        into technicalities unless the assessor asked for it.
